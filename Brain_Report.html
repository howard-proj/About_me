The Importance of Data Evaluation in Neurophysiologic Analysis

Executive Summary¶

Sleep studies have shown that poor sleeping patterns correlate to poorer future mental health in individuals. Therefore today, it's more relevant than ever to study changing sleep stages which constitute these sleep patterns as well as methods used to detect these sleep stages, including the electrooculogram (EOG) often used to detect REM sleep. However, although such techniques conducted in controlled scientific environments can accurately track a subject through the stages of sleep, in uncontrolled environments it's often difficult to attain the same level of preciseness, mostly due to variation in calibration and environmental noise. This limits the people who can study these phenomena, thus limiting scientific development.

Therefore, through software that can visualise varying input parameters on output metrics, thereby testing the model's robustness to noise, users are provided with an invaluable tool to attain near optimal parameters given some signal with its implied range of environmental variation. To obtain the near optimal parameters, a configuration region of interest should be specified by the user to then randomly iterate within this space, yielding performance of the configurated model at each step.

The software was implemented towards signal isolation of eye movement events from an EOG. However, the concept may be generalised to include any time sequential event isolation problems, albeit requiring it's own ground truth generation algorithm. In this case, due to resource constraints, the concept was demonstrated using an SVM classifier alongside four input hyperparameters: classification window, sample proportion, cleaner window and cleaner threshold.

Introduction¶

Polysomnography (PSG) is a form of sleep study that categorises sleeping patterns in two stages, Rapid Eye Movement (REM) sleep or Non-Rapid Eye Movement (NREM) sleep. With a recent study showing 60% of Australians reporting at least one sleep symptom occurring three or more times per week, neupherological techniques such as polysomnography can be used in identifying and diagnosing such disorders. In aiming to identify and diagnose sleep disorders such as sleep apnea, narcolepsy and periodic limb movements, different physiologic signals can be recorded such as eye movements using an electrooculogram (EOG) (Mayo Clinic, 2018). In EOG, two electrodes are placed on the right and left outer canthus to record horizontal eye movements that are picked up as inherent voltage, as the cornea has a positive charge and retina has a negative charge (Armon, C., 2020). By documenting these eye movements, REM sleep can be monitored in evaluating such sleeping disorders. A clinical study conducted in 2014 aimed to evaluate the importance of neurophysiologic techniques i.e. sleep recordings in patients with prolonged disorders of consciousness (De Biase, S., et al. 2014). Results showed that patients with sleep recordings displayed better clinical scores than those who didn't, with REM sleep being the only sleep element achieving statistical significance. Using multivariate analysis, REM sleep was associated with higher Coma Recovery Scale-Revised (CRS-R) scores, proving PSG records to be a reliable tool in conducting a neurophysiological assessment in patients with such sleeping disorders.

The key problems in polysomnography data analysis include poor, noisy data quality, the selection of relevant information and the classification process itself. These three problems are explored using REM data in focusing mainly on the significant features extracted. Also known as the dreaming state, REM occurs when brain waves are most active during sleep and can be quantitatively classified as between 7 to 23 eye movements per minute in which anything above and below this range is considered wakefulness or other NREM stages respectively. Using a spikerbox from Backyard Brains, these stages of wakefulness, REM and NREM can be recorded using the same proven parameters. By replicating a PSG tool using a spikerbox, data analytics can be explored in understanding how various parameters contribute to neurophysiological conclusions such as predicting REM and NREM sleep. The emphasis on the interdisciplinary approach ensures that the main issues in current neurophysiological tools being biased to different sleep stages is monitored, dedicating the central focus of this project on extracting descriptive features from a collection of PSG data (Chaberova, J., 2017).

Section 1: Data Collection and Classifcation Process¶

REM sleep data is filtered and saved as a waveform data. This data is filtered using a Gaussian Filter, a specific Fast Fourier transform filter method. “Fast Fourier transform plays a key role in physics because it can be used as a mathematical tool to describe the relationship between the time domain and frequency domain representation of discrete signals” (Nussbaumer 1981). This way, waveform data is converted from time domain into frequency domain where appropriate analysis can be performed. Gaussian filter is applied for noise suppression where the noise is smoothed out, at the same time the signal is distorted (Cahiill, Deng 1993). Through these two filtrations, waveform features can be extracted.

“Measures of central tendency tells researchers where the center value lies in the distribution of data” (Allen 2017). Central tendencies collect features in waveform data to find the presence of eye movements after noise suppression in the frequency domain. These are further equipped with the presence of supervised machine learning and continuous eye movements. Supervised machine learning classifiers are used in REM sleep analysis as instances in waveform data can be labelled as eye movement or not. Waveform data are trained and tested after the measure of central tendency to make reliable future predictions. Continuous eye movements remove anomalous predictions and count contiguous predictions from these classifiers to count the number of eye movements present.

Evaluations of classifiers are measured with F1 score. This is because “harmonic mean of precision and recall makes F1 score to be widely used as a measure of success of a binary classifier” (Zachary 2014).Classifiers and F1 scores are computed through Python Scikit-learn library. This library is well suited for statistical data analysis by non-specialists in the software and web industries, as well in the fields outside computer science, such as biology or physics. In particular, the accuracy yielded by F1 score that calculates number of eye movements will be used as an intermediate that measures different parameters interactions upon analysing the waveform data.

In maintaining reproducibility, the classifier of interest is Support Vector Machine (SVM) based on the data type and the preprocessing methods. SVM is a supervised machine learning algorithm that finds the optimal hyperplane of the variable margin size to separate and classify classes of data points in feature space. The standard four parameters of Classification Window Size, Sample Proportion, Cleaner Window, Cleaner Threshold and the extra parameter, SVM cost can be explained in terms of their usage within the SVM classifier. Classification window size sets the size of the window which scans through the data and in which classifications are performed. Sample proportion can speed up the classification latency for large sets of data by only running through a chosen proportion of the entire data. Cleaner Window takes a chosen number of consecutive classification windows and identifies an eye movement if the ratio of positive events to the number of chosen windows is greater than the Cleaner Threshold, which is a number between 0 and 1. Finally, SVM cost is the extra classifier parameter that allows you to adjust how large (soft) or narrow (hard) your margin classification should be. For example, decreasing cost will correspond to increasing margin and will be more flexible with misclassifications.

Pre-recorded datas is collected by Arduino and saved as a wav file. These datas are filtered using Fast Fourier Transform. FFT filter is used to make the data into a function of frequency. Once datas is filtered, central tendencies features are collected through the wav files to get any movements in the data. Presence of continuous eye movements can be encountered through the use of window sequence cleaner. This cleaner removes anomalous predictions and counts contiguous predictions in the extracted data to find the number of eye movements present.

def iteratePipeline(randomParameters, classifierType = None):
    for i, parameters in enumerate(randomParameters):
        if classifierType is None:
            classifierType = random.choice(classifierTypes)
        print('==== Iteration: {} out of {} ===='.format(i+1, len(randomParameters)))
        analysisParameters = {
            "classifierType": classifierType,
            "classifierWindow": parameters[0],
            "classifierProportion": parameters[1],
            "cleanerWindow": parameters[2],
            "cleanerProportion": parameters[3],
        }
        print('Parameter:', analysisParameters)
        analysisParameters["classifierStep"] = 100
        if classifierType == "nn" or classifierType == "rf":
            analysisParameters["extraParameter"] = int(parameters[4])
        elif classifierType == "svm":
            analysisParameters["extraParameter"] = parameters[4]

        user = doPipe(analysisParameters)
    return

def generateRandomParameters(analysisParameters):
    iterNum = analysisParameters['iterationNum']
    outputParameters = []

    for k in range(iterNum):
        windowSize = round(random.uniform(
            analysisParameters['classifierWindow'][0],
            analysisParameters['classifierWindow'][1]
        ))
        sampleProportion = round(random.uniform(
            analysisParameters['classifierProportion'][0],
            analysisParameters['classifierProportion'][1]
        ),3)
        cynthiaWindow = round(random.uniform(
            analysisParameters['cleanerWindow'][0],
            analysisParameters['cleanerWindow'][1]
        ))
        cynthiaThreshold = round(random.uniform(
            analysisParameters['cleanerProportion'][0],
            analysisParameters['cleanerProportion'][1]
        ), 2)

        if analysisParameters['extraParameter'] is not None:
            extraParameter = round(random.uniform(
                analysisParameters['extraParameter'][0],
                analysisParameters['extraParameter'][1]
            ),2)
            parameterRow = [windowSize, sampleProportion, cynthiaWindow, cynthiaThreshold, extraParameter]
        else:
            parameterRow = [windowSize, sampleProportion, cynthiaWindow, cynthiaThreshold]
        
        outputParameters.append(parameterRow)

    return outputParameters

In addition the Cleaner threshold acts as a value to control the entire model’s sensitivity to events classified across contiguous frames of time. Higher thresholds, too, increase time delay and therefore lower values are preferred from the aspect of time. However too low causes overcounting. It acts as a buffer to the Cleaner Window, increasing tolerance to anomalous prediction sequences.

As SVM cost was the only parameter that illustrated no trends, the optimal conclusions from the remaining parameters can be deduced. For larger Classification Windows, the counts converge to 0, as a result of being exposed to more noise and the characteristics are overpowered by this noise. As Sample Proportion increases, the variation in count decreases to 3 due to larger sample proportion decreasing the overfitting. A higher sample proportion is therefore better by limited by computational resources. A higher Cleaner Window in short files is preferable to get rid of anomalous noise and reduce overcounting. However the longer eye movement files include narrowly spaced movements that result in being merged and cleaned along with the anomalous noise reducing overcounting. Finally, the variance of the short file reduces with a higher threshold as it decreases tolerance to short events so the mean count decreases and reduces overcounting. For longer file however, the variance reduces with lower threshold as it increases tolerance to short events so the mean count increases and therefore reducing undercounting.

Deployment¶

In obtaining the optimal parameters and results, the code developed above can be implemented as a product. The development process consisted of the Flask package and javascript in creating a html based web app. Flask is a lightweight WSGI web application framework that provides functionality for building web applications (Smyth, P., 2020). By running HTTP requests in Python functions after users interact with the application, results are returned through the web app. Tasks were assigned to students according to their skills. Students who had the knowledge wrote python code while the rest worked on improving the user interface. As results were conducted and developed in python scripts, the performance and loading tests were tested during the deployment of the web application. Finally, usability and compatibility testing among different users was refined and improved in producing the most suitable outcome.

The final product is an interactive web application that aims to educate the importance of data analysis on brain wave data (Appendix 1). The application contains three processes: inputting the data, assigning parameters and visualising the output. Users are required to upload a wav file from their server that contains at least three seconds of data. Once the file has been loaded, users must choose a classifier out of the nine options to focus their analysis on and are able to choose specific values for parameters or ranges for randomised analysis. All classifiers have the general parameters mentioned above and in addition, the SVM, Random Forest and kNN classifier have specialised parameters of Cost and K value respectively. Once clicking calculate, the output stage includes a graph of the prediction of eye movements, the accuracy of the classification, the number of eye movements predicted. Furthermore, if a signal analysis is conducted, this data point is added to a 4D plot that compares the axis of the users choosing with accuracy adding the 4th dimension of a heat map. If random data is produced, the number of points created in the iteration will be added on the graph. Finally, all data points are stored in an interactive table that saves the inputs and outputs as well as classifying if the file contains REM sleep. By allowing users to alter and store the variations of parameters on classifying eye movements, evaluation of PSG recordings becomes more time effective and accurate.

The feature of randomisation within the product aims to minimize confounding factors which are common causes of exposure and outcome (Pourhoseinghoi, Baghestani, Vahedi 2012). By multiple iterations and testing, the insignificant parameters can be determined and eliminated as they do not not contribute significantly to the overall accuracy in counting REM sleep. The analysis above demonstrates the process that randomisation best removes confounding factors at the design stage of the research.

Discussion¶

The advantage of our app is the visualisation and interactive nature it holds as it can illustrate clear trends in the evaluation metrics using a 2D graph and 4D graph alongside a history table of parameters and evaluation metrics. Due to the various input parameters and high-dimensional space, visualisation can be difficult, in which graphs were used in reducing dimensionality, facilitated by the fact that other variables are selected in a uniform distribution, thus serving as background noise. Thus the resulting distribution of output metrics will preserve correlation to the input parameters. This assumes there is a linear relationship between input parameters and output metrics. If the parameters interact in a non-linear way, this randomisation could distort the output trends that are flattened to a lower dimension. Another shortcoming of the app is computing complexity. Each updated parameter requires potentially an entirely new classification model and therefore iterations over this may require many degrees of complexity, making the process slow. In order to do the thousands of iterations, a large time frame is required to sufficiently populate the high dimensional space and obtain accurate results.

Furthermore due to this required population of datapoints, it often overcrowds to the point of reduced visibility of trends on lower dimensional graphs. Therefore some method of exploration of these populations with greater resolution would be desired and adapted into future work, for example a filter that could narrow down the visible space to just the most optimal hyperparameter regions. Another shortcoming is that our app currently isn’t tested on real sleeping data. Although the app would theoretically work just the same, it has not been tested. To improve this, we can use real sleep data that contains different stages of sleep to give a more realistic result. In addition, we can also combine it with various resources to produce significant results that form trends with accuracy in determining stronger, effective conclusions. Furthermore, as sleep gets more attention in the sleep tech market, forcased to be worth around $80 billion by the end of 2020, the relevance of this analysis remains essential (Dream, 2020).

Conclusion¶

The importance of the application developed is illustrated between Section 1 and Section 3 of the results as the optimal parameters and ranges deduced from randomisation improved the prediction of eye movements and its accuracy. Of the 5 parameters analysed, the extra parameter of SVM cost illustrated insignificant results therefore concluding a higher importance on the other four parameters. While these results showed dependency on the length of the file, other limitations and potentially future study may include analysing other dependencies that contributed to the final result. Ultimately our app can be used to improve the performance of eye movement detection which can aid in the study of REM sleep. In catering to a wide target audience such as students and researchers, another area of future work can include adapting the application and results on other signal isolation problems beyond REM sleep and neurological tools such as polysomnography.
